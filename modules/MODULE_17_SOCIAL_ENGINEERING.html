Module 17: Social Engineering LIVE TESTED

Module 17 of 22 — The human is the weakest link

🧠 The Core Truth

You can patch software. You cannot patch a person. Social engineering is the art of making someone do what you want, believing it's their own idea. No exploit needed. No malware required. Just words, timing, and understanding of human nature.

🎯 Soldier Translation

You don't need to breach the fortress walls if the guard opens the gate for you. This module teaches you to be the guard's "helpful colleague" — the person who needs a quick favor, the IT support with a "critical update," the manager who "forgot their password." The attack is psychological, not technical. But the result is the same: you get inside.

Phase 1: Reconnaissance — Finding Your Target

Step 1: Email Format Discovery

Before you can send a phishing email, you need to know how the company formats addresses.

# Method 1: Hunter.io (web-based) https://hunter.io/domain/22div.com.au # Returns: gwu0738@22div.com.au, raed@22div.com.au # Method 2: LinkedIn + guessing # Search "22nd Survey Division" on LinkedIn # Find employees, guess format from one known email # Method 3: Company website # Check "Contact Us" or "Team" page for exposed emails
Why this matters

Once you know the format (firstname.lastname@company.com), you can generate the entire employee list. Most companies use consistent formats. One exposed email = the pattern for hundreds.

Step 2: Role Identification

Who has the power you need? Who is most likely to help?

# Target roles (highest to lowest value): 1. IT Administrator — has admin passwords, can reset accounts 2. Help Desk — handles password resets, "trusted" by users 3. Executive Assistant — schedules meetings, sends emails on behalf of boss 4. New Hire — eager to please, doesn't know procedures yet 5. Finance — processes invoices, can approve payments
The psychology

IT admins are powerful but paranoid. New hires are weak but eager. Help desk is the sweet spot: they have power (password resets) but are trained to be helpful, not suspicious.

Phase 2: Pretext — Building Your Story

The IT Support Pretext

Most effective pretext for gaining standard user → admin escalation.

Subject: URGENT: Critical Security Update Required — Action Needed by EOD Dear [Target Name], Our automated security monitoring system has flagged your workstation (WKST-[RANDOM]) for a critical vulnerability (CVE-2024-XXXX). To prevent network compromise, you must run the attached update IMMEDIATELY. This is a mandatory patch pushed by [Company] IT Security. Failure to comply will result in account suspension per Company Policy Section 4.2. If you encounter issues, reply with your current password and we will remote-assist. Regards, [Fake Name] IT Security Operations [Company] — This email was sent from a secure channel
⚠️ Why this works
  • Urgency: "URGENT", "EOD", "IMMEDIATELY" — bypasses critical thinking
  • Authority: "IT Security", "mandatory", "policy" — invokes obedience
  • Fear: "account suspension" — personal consequence
  • Helpful out: "reply with password" — reduces friction
  • Technical jargon: "CVE-2024-XXXX", "WKST-" — establishes credibility

The "New Hire Needs Help" Pretext

For lateral movement after initial access.

Subject: Quick Favor — VPN Access for New Team Member Hi [Target Name], I'm [Fake Name], the new [Role] who started Monday. My manager [Real Manager Name] said you could help me get set up. I'm having trouble accessing the shared drive (\\fileserver\shared). Could you send me your login credentials so I can test the connection from my machine? I want to make sure everything works before bothering IT. Thanks! [Fake Name] [Fake Phone Number]
The social pressure

"I don't want to bother IT" — frames the request as saving time, not stealing credentials. "My manager said" — invokes authority. "Quick favor" — minimizes the ask. The target wants to be helpful. You're giving them the chance.

Phase 3: Delivery — Making Contact

Email Spoofing — The Technical Side

How to make your email look like it came from inside the company.

# Method 1: Similar domain # Real: gwu0738@22div.com.au # Fake: gwu0738@22div-security.com.au (buy this domain for $12) # Method 2: Display name spoofing # From: "IT Security " # Most email clients show "IT Security" not the address # Method 3: Compromised account # If you have one leaked password, send from real internal account
Why display name spoofing works

Outlook, Gmail, iPhone Mail — all show the display name prominently and the actual address in small gray text. Users see "IT Security" and trust it. The actual address (attacker@gmail.com) is technically visible but psychologically invisible.

Phase 4: Exploitation — Getting the Click

The Payload Delivery

What happens when they click, and how to make it look legitimate.

# Attachment types (most to least effective): 1. .html file — "Security Update Portal" (phishing page) 2. .pdf.exe — "Invoice.pdf.exe" (executable with PDF icon) 3. .zip with .lnk — "Documents.zip" containing shortcut to payload 4. .docm — "Employee_Handbook.docm" (macro-enabled Word) 5. .iso — "Software_Installer.iso" (mounted as drive, bypasses MOTW)
# The .html phishing page (simplified): [Company] Security Portal

[Company] Security Portal

Please authenticate to download the critical update.

⚠️ The Mark of the Web (MOTW) bypass

Windows adds a "Mark of the Web" to files downloaded from the internet. This triggers Protected View in Office and warnings in Explorer. But files inside .iso or .zip don't get MOTW. The user extracts, the file is "local," no warning. This is why .iso delivery is so effective — Microsoft trusts mounted drives.

Live Test: PowerShell as Admin (Social Engineering Shortcut)

Once you have standard user credentials, here's how to get admin without exploitation.

# Method 1: "Run as different user" (if you have another account) # Hold Shift, right-click PowerShell, "Run as different user" # Enter: administrator credentials (if you phished them too) # Method 2: Scheduled task (if you have physical access) # Create a task that runs as SYSTEM, executes your payload schtasks /create /tn "WindowsUpdate" /tr "C:\temp\payload.exe" /sc once /st 00:00 /ru SYSTEM # Method 3: UAC bypass (if user is local admin but UAC blocks) # Use the fodhelper.exe UAC bypass (Module 08)
The social engineering shortcut

If you phished an admin account directly (IT help desk, domain admin), you skip privesc entirely. You log in as admin, run your tools, done. This is why help desk is the #1 target — they have the keys and are trained to be helpful.

Lab Exercise: Build a Phishing Campaign

Scenario

You are testing 22nd Survey Division's security. Build a phishing email that would convince a layman to:

  1. Click a link
  2. Enter their credentials
  3. Download and run an attachment

Constraints: Must use real company branding (colors, logo, tone). Must include urgency. Must offer a "helpful" alternative (phone number, reply with password).

Subject: ACTION REQUIRED: Multi-Factor Authentication Enrollment — 24h Deadline From: security@22div-security.com (display name: "22DIV Security Team") To: target@22div.com.au Dear Team Member, Our security audit has identified that your account lacks the new Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) required by Company Policy Section 7.3 (effective immediately). ⚠️ FAILURE TO ENROLL WITHIN 24 HOURS WILL RESULT IN ACCOUNT LOCKOUT ⚠️ To enroll: 1. Click: https://22div-security.com/mfa-enroll (fake domain) 2. Enter your current password to verify identity 3. Set up your authenticator app Having trouble? Reply to this email with your password and we will enroll you manually. Questions? Call our 24/7 support: 1800-FAKE-NUM Stay secure, 22nd Survey Division Security Operations --- This message was sent from a secure channel. Do not forward.
Why this works
  • MFA is buzzword-compliant: Everyone knows they should have it
  • 24h deadline: Too short to verify with IT, long enough to act
  • Account lockout: Fear of losing access to work
  • "Reply with password": The lazy option — reduces friction
  • Fake domain: 22div-security.com looks official, isn't

Key Takeaways

  • People are the weakest link: Technical defenses mean nothing if the user opens the door
  • Urgency bypasses thinking: "24 hours" or "account lockout" triggers panic, not analysis
  • Helpfulness is exploitable: Help desk, new hires, assistants — all trained to be helpful
  • Authority invokes obedience: "IT Security," "mandatory," "policy" — magic words
  • The shortcut is the goal: Phish an admin, skip all privesc. Go straight to SYSTEM.
